Drug-related deaths, heroin, benzodiazepines and Harm Reduction in Scotland

Definition of drug and problems with them

To start this is research, it is necessary and essential to start with a definition of the word “drug”. From the Cambridge dictionary, the following definitions are going to be exposed:

      1. “Any natural or artificially made chemical that is used as a medicine.”
      2. “Any natural or artificially made chemical that is taken for pleasure, to improve someone's performance of an activity, or because a person cannot stop using it”
      3. “To give a person or animal a chemical that causes him, her, or it to lose feeling or become unconscious”.

From these definitions, the second one is the more adequate for the purpose of this research. It can further narrow down the term, defining “drugs” as:

“Any natural or artificially made chemical that is taken for pleasure or because a person cannot stop using it.”

From this definition, the need arises to establish when this “substance that is taken” becomes a problem. If this definition is carefully analysed, the chemical substance doesn’t seem to be a problem by itself, but the consequences for the user and for those who, not having used it, or actively participated in the production, trafficking or any process related to this substance, are caused a consequence related to the user or those who participated actively.

Thus, we will use the Scottish Government's official approach to this term. It is said that the official definitions focus the attention on the prolonged use of particular drugs. So, Scottish Affair Committee (Problem drug use in Scotland, 2019), provides two brief definitions for “drug-use problem”:

      1. From NHS Health Scotland: “use of opioids [...] and/or the illicit use of benzodiazepines, and implies routine and prolonged use as opposed to recreational and occasional drug use”.
      2. Another definition: “use of any drug which is causing medical, social, psychological, physical, financial or legal problems”

Thus, for the purposes of this paper, a drug use problem will be defined as: "undesirable medical, social, psychological, physical, financial or legal consequences of long-term substance use, both for the user and the non-user". Here it is necessary to emphasise that it is not only those who use these substances on a long-term basis who suffer the consequences of their use, taking into account the sphere surrounding the consumer, as it is the reason for this research to analyse the policies followed by the Scottish government to alleviate these consequences. Occasional use, usually called recreational use, is then ruled out.

Another question worth highlighting and analysing is what are the causes of these behaviours that trigger undesirable consequences and whether they are taken into account by the relevant authorities.

In concluding this section, we will consider a number of substances that fall into the category of drugs that this research aims to analyse. Crew Scotland, a charity that attempts “to reduce harm, challenge perceptions and help people make positive choices about their use of cannabis, stimulant and other drugs”, lists a number of substances and their categories:

      1. Depressants: Alcohol, Benzodiazepines, Etizolam, GHB/GBL, Xanax (alprazolam)
      2. Cannabinoids: Cannabis, CBD
      3. Dissociatives: Ketamine, Nitrous Oxide
      4. Empathogens: MDMA (Ecstasy)
      5. Opioids: Fentanyl, Heroin, Naloxone
      6. Psychedelics: 2C family, DMT, LSD, Magic mushrooms
      7. Stimulants: Amphetamine (speed), Cocaine, Methamphetamine

However, for reasons explained below and in order not to make the research too lengthy, only the following substances will be considered for this investigation:

  • From depressants: Benzodiazepines
  • Opioids

Read more about the different kind of drugs: https://www.crew.scot/drugs-information/
