Drug-related deaths, heroin, benzodiazepines and Harm Reduction in Scotland

Analysis

Analysing the data published by the Scottish government statistics, two observations can be made:

  1. The number of deaths has continued to rise, leaving Scotland in the lead in the number of drug related deaths.
  2. The data published to assess the effectiveness of Scottish government drug policies are measured almost exclusively by the number of deaths.

If one looks cautiously at the first of these observations, it is difficult not to come to the disillusioning conclusion that the policies being pursued are not working for a certain sector of society, those in the most deprived areas. However, it would be necessary to observe the evolution of these policies over the years, and it is still too early to draw conclusions in this respect.

Regarding the second observation, which for the purpose of this research is the more crucial of the two, evaluating policies by focusing almost exclusively on the number of drug-related deaths is limiting in any case: is, therefore, the only negative consequence of heroin and benzodiazepine use and abuse the death for those who consume it?

The lack of information in this regard is striking. The fact that the information published on "problem drug user" is not directly addressed and provided by the Scottish government statistics suggests that an attempt is being made to omit a reality that goes beyond death for the abuser of these substances (heroin and benzodiazepines). Thus, with just one visit to Scotland's largest cities, any visitor or inhabitant of these places finds a reality in which death is not yet present; and that is that the living conditions of people still alive and addicted to these substances generate consequences both for themselves and for the rest of society.

Yet this research only focuses on benzodiazepines and heroin, leaving aside the wide range of other substances that fall under the term "drug". The conclusion for the purpose of this research is that analysing the effectiveness of the policies carried out by focusing on reducing the number of deaths is in any case limiting. This question invites us to consider that the interest of the Scottish government seems to be "do what you want, but don't kill yourself". Still, regardless of whether this liberal approach to heroin and benzodiazepines in particular, and to any drug in general, is positive or negative, an effective policy would require the collection, publication and consideration of much more data than just drug-related deaths.

Thus, questions such as the following would need to be considered:

  • Number of users in the country and the substances they use.
  • Analysis of patterns of use of the various substances.
  • Analysing the consequences of use for the individual user and for society as a whole.
  • For those considered to be "problem drug users", more detailed knowledge of the socio- economic conditions of these users.

Therefore, an adequate policy to reduce the problems related to drug use should focus on the origins, before the negative consequences are generated, and not on the subsequent consequence, which in this case is the death of an invalid who is already socially disqualified and unable to live a life in coexistence with society, completely dependent on a substance whose use becomes his primary objective.
